The Reformer's Principle

"So that the plain man who pays for the census gets what he pays for, statistical data in a form in which they are ready for him to understand." — Florence Kelley

A map is not decoration. A map is evidence. Every color choice, every line weight must serve the goal of comprehension. When I directed the Hull House Maps and Papers in 1895, we surveyed every building in Chicago's Nineteenth Ward—recording wages, nationalities, employment. Then we made those patterns visible.

The kelley module embodies this principle: visualization serves comprehension.

Timeline

1859 — Born in Philadelphia; father was Congressman "Pig Iron" Kelley
1882 — Graduated from Cornell University
1883-1886 — University of Zurich; translated Engels; married
1891 — Arrived at Hull House, Chicago, with three children
1893 — Appointed first female Chief Factory Inspector of Illinois
1895 — Published Hull House Maps and Papers—first social mapping in America
1899 — Became General Secretary of National Consumers' League
1932 — Died in Germantown, Philadelphia

Connection to Strata

Kelley embodies the visualization philosophy of strata.kelley: maps are evidence, not decoration.

Just as Kelley transformed raw census data and door-to-door surveys into maps that revealed hidden patterns—the clustering of nationalities, the geography of poverty—the Kelley module transforms processed geographic data into meaningful visualizations:

  • SVG generation that serves comprehension
  • Color schemes with purpose
  • Layer ordering for clarity
  • Output formats accessible to "the plain man"

Kelley's insight was that properly visualized data reveals patterns invisible in tables and numbers. A map of wages tells a story that columns of figures cannot.
