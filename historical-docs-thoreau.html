The Surveyor's Principle

"It is remarkable how long men will believe in the bottomlessness of a pond without taking the trouble to sound it. I surveyed it carefully, before the ice broke up, early in '46, with compass and chain and sounding line." — Henry David Thoreau, Walden

I have walked to the Census Bureau, so to speak, and returned with their TIGER files—those careful delineations of every county, town, and water body in the nation. The work of acquisition is simple but requires patience. One must know where to look, and one must go to the source rather than accept hearsay.

When I measured Walden Pond, I did not ask others for their estimates. I went out upon the ice with my sounding line and measured it myself. The same principle guides the thoreau module: fetch authoritative data directly.

Connection to Strata

Thoreau embodies the data acquisition philosophy of strata.thoreau: go to the source. Just as Thoreau would not accept hearsay about Walden Pond's depth but went out with his sounding line to measure it himself, the Thoreau module fetches data directly from authoritative sources—the Census Bureau's TIGER files, Quebec's open data portal, Canada's NHN database.

The surveyor's practical wisdom applies: don't speculate about boundaries when you can fetch the authoritative shapefile.
