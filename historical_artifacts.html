William Smith

23 March 1769 – 28 August 1839

strata (core package)

The Foundation: Layer Ordering and Superposition

Known as the "Father of English Geology," Smith was a self-taught canal surveyor who discovered that rock strata could be identified by their fossil contents. His 1815 geological map of England and Wales was the first detailed geological map of any country. The package name "Strata" directly honors his fundamental insight: layer order matters.

"The same strata were found always in the same order of superposition and contained the same peculiar fossils."
— William Smith

Henry David Thoreau

12 July 1817 – 6 May 1862

strata.thoreau

Data Acquisition: Fetching from Authoritative Sources

A professional land surveyor who created over 200 surveys including detailed bathymetric maps of Walden Pond. Thoreau's meticulous approach to direct observation—going to the source rather than accepting hearsay—embodies the philosophy of the thoreau module: fetch authoritative data directly.

"It is remarkable how long men will believe in the bottomlessness of a pond without taking the trouble to sound it."
— Henry David Thoreau, Walden

Alexander von Humboldt

14 September 1769 – 6 May 1859

strata.humboldt

Processing & Transformation: Revealing Hidden Connections

The "Father of Ecology" who invented isothermal lines and thematic mapping. Humboldt's approach—collect observations, compare and combine them, reveal hidden connections—defines the humboldt module: transform raw data to reveal patterns.

"The most important aim of all physical science is this: to recognize unity in diversity."
— Alexander von Humboldt

Matthew Fontaine Maury

14 January 1806 – 1 February 1873

strata.maury

Pipeline Orchestration: Coordinating Many Sources

The "Pathfinder of the Seas" and "Father of Oceanography" who collected ship logs from captains worldwide, aggregating thousands of observations into comprehensive wind and current charts. His approach defines the maury module: coordinate many sources to reveal patterns invisible to individuals.

"There is a river in the ocean. In the severest droughts it never fails, and in the mightiest floods it never overflows."
— Matthew Fontaine Maury, on the Gulf Stream

Florence Kelley

12 September 1859 – 17 February 1932

strata.kelley

Visualization: Maps as Evidence, Not Decoration

A social reformer who directed the Hull House Maps and Papers (1895)—the first systematic spatial investigation of an American neighborhood. Her color-coded maps of wages and nationalities made hidden patterns visible to reformers and legislators. The kelley module embodies her principle: visualization serves comprehension.

"So that the plain man who pays for the census gets what he pays for, statistical data in a form in which they are ready for him to understand."
— Florence Kelley

Maria Mitchell

1 August 1818 – 28 June 1889

strata.tui

Interactive Wizard: Guided Observation for New Users

America's first professional female astronomer and Vassar College's first astronomy professor. Mitchell discovered a comet in 1847 through systematic telescopic observation, becoming internationally famous. Her patient, methodical teaching style—guiding students step-by-step to the telescope eyepiece—embodies the TUI's approach: systematic guidance reveals what random looking misses.

"Do not look at stars as bright spots only. Try to take in the vastness of the universe."
— Maria Mitchell
