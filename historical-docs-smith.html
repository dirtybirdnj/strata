William Smith

23 March 1769 — 28 August 1839

Father of English Geology • Creator of the First Geological Map of a Nation

strata (core package)

The Founding Principle

"The same strata were found always in the same order of superposition and contained the same peculiar fossils. Organized fossils are to the naturalist as coins to the antiquary; they are the antiquities of the earth." — William Smith, 1816

I was but a canal surveyor when I made my discovery. In the cuttings of the Somerset Coal Canal, where the exposed rock faces revealed what no book could teach, I observed that the same strata were found always in the same order of superposition. Each layer distinct, identifiable by its fossils, and positioned in proper relation to those above and below.

This same principle—that order matters, that layers must be properly superposed—applies with equal force to the construction of maps. The package that bears my name embodies this philosophy: layer order matters.

Timeline

1769 — Born in Churchill, Oxfordshire
1787 — Apprenticed to Edward Webb, surveyor, in Stow-on-the-Wold
1793-1794 — Surveys Somerset Coal Canal; discovers principle of faunal succession
1799 — Dictates first "Order of the Strata" at the Swan Inn, Dunkerton
1815 — Publishes "A Delineation of the Strata of England and Wales" — the first geological map of a nation
1819 — Imprisoned for debt; loses collection and property
1831 — Receives first Wollaston Medal; named "Father of English Geology"
1839 — Dies in Northampton, buried at St Peter's Church

Connection to Strata

William Smith embodies the core principle of the Strata package: layer order matters. Just as Smith discovered that rock layers appear in a consistent order determined by geological processes, our cartographic layers must be ordered correctly—backgrounds at bottom, water bodies above, boundaries with cutouts next, and roads at the very top.

The package name "Strata" directly honors Smith's fundamental insight: understanding comes from recognizing and respecting the natural ordering of layers.
